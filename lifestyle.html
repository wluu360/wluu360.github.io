Lifestyle

LIFE IN WATER

Earlier ichthyosaurs were undulating creatures with long bodies and swam similarly to eels. This motion can be described as wave-like. As they evolved, their bodies began to closely resemble that of a fish, such as a tuna, even eventually acquiring a dorsal fin. Its body was designed to swim very fast, and was capable of reaching speeds up to 40 km/h.

It had a very powerful tail that allowed it to travel at great speeds, and had fins that were small and stiff that were like paddles, which let it maneuver up and down in the water. Although the ichthyosaur lived in the water, they did not have gills that allowed them to breath underwater. As a result, the ichthyosaur relied on periodic surfacing to obtain air, much like modern whales today. Ichthyosaurs possessed much smaller red blood cells compared to a modern day mammal in order to increase the efficiency of oxygen intake. The size difference was nearly 5 times smaller than that of a human. This occurrence was likely due to the low atmospheric oxygen content during the Jurassic period.



BIRTH

Although many modern reptiles give birth to eggs, that was not the case for Ichthyosaurs. The birth method of ichthyosaurs was unknown for a long time until a fossil was found in South Majiashan, China in 2011. The fossil was of a Ichthyosaur mother and was found to be carrying at least three offspring, with the second offspring half way out of the birth canal. This discovery revealed that ichthyosaurs not only had live-birth but gave birth head first, similar to most land animals.

Diet

Just as many animals consume nutrients to survive, ichthyosaurus are not an exception. These prehistoric marine reptiles were strictly carnivores. The diet of ichthyosaurus mostly consisted of fish, with some reptiles and ancient cephalopods also included in their feeding; however, the range of their diet depended on the morphology of the different types of ichthyosaurus.

For instance, some ichthyosaurus had pointed snouts, such as the genus Eurhinosaurus, which led to the hunting of smaller animals. These species of ichthyosaurus were speculated to use their snouts to slash prey, akin to modern day swordfish. Most ichthyosaurus also contained large eyes suggesting that these predators hunted at night or at great depths. Evidence to suggest this is shown in modern day large or colossal squids, in which their anatomy contains large eyes that aid in hunting at specific locations at night.

Some species were even able to hunt prey of its own size. These ichthyosaurus contained large bladed teeth and were known as macro predators: predators that are fairly large to the naked eye. Some examples of these Ichthyosaurus are found in the genus of Thalattoarchon, Himalaysaurus, and several species in the Temnodontosaurus.

Evidence for these findings have been discovered through coprolites and fossilized stomach contents of ichthyosaurs. Coprolites are fossilized feces which, when identified, were found to contain the remains of specific diets of the species it pertained to. Another evidence for their diet was found in fossilized stomach contents. These remains contained large amounts of fossilized cephalopod beaks, scale remnants, partly digested fish, and even Ichthyosaurus.
