Environment





Ichthyosaurs are Mesozoic marine reptiles, and they are often referred to as fish-lizards. They were widespread across the Northern hemisphere in the Early Triassic, and were also found in the Southern hemisphere by Late Triassic. They were cold-blooded, therefore relied on external heat to maintain their internal temperature and living in water may have been disadvantageous since it is more efficient at removing heat than air. However, they were still suitable to live in aquatic environments more than mammals and birds, as long as surface water temperature was about 20°C.

Ichthyosaurs had extremely large eyes, which is believed to be an adaptation for low-light environments especially at deeper depths because having larger eyes meant that they were able to collect or gather larger amounts of light. On top of that, because ichthyosaurs evolved a fish-like form, they were able to not only swim fast and far but also dive more deeply, especially knowing that they also had large eyes to accommodate for the low light environment at deeper depths. However, because they needed to swim to the surface for air, they could not have lived too deep in the ocean.

Therefore, considering the combination of all these defining features, they have often been associated to living in the pelagic zone, where they are neither close to the bottom nor near the shore of the ocean.












Fact:

In far northwestern Nye County, Nevada, there is a state park called
Berlin-Ichthyosaur State Park which is a public recreation area and historic preserve that protects undisturbed Ichthyosaur fossils.












In the vicinity of the Tyndall Glacier in Torres del Paine National Park of Southern Chile, there have been found abundant and very well preserved remnants of ichthyosaurs.

The Tyndall Glacier represents one of the fossil Lagerstätten for Early Cretaceous marine reptiles worldwide. It is believed that so many remnants of ichthyosaurs can be found in this region because Tyndall ichthyosaurs benefitted from the cold upwelling currents that allowed for abundant life at the shelf edge and that included abundance of their preferred diet of belemnites and small fish.

Also, many pregnant females, neonates, juveniles and adult ichthyosaurs have been found in the Tyndall deposit, suggesting that the upslope environment was beneficial in terms of food and reproduction.
