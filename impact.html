Impact





In May 2016, a discovery was made of an Ichthyosaur fossil on the beach at Lilstock, Somerset (UK), which included several pieces of bone that together measured one meter in length. After analysis, it was determined to represent an incomplete bone from the lower jaw of a giant Ichthyosaur.

This discovery was significant because this bone was compared to four other similar bones that were previously found and identified as part of a dinosaur. Through this comparison, it was realized that those previously found bones were not actually of a dinosaur but in fact, were also jaw fragments of giant Ichthyosaurs.

The bone of the Ichthyosaur that was discovered in Lilstock has allowed us to discover past mistakes and misidentifications, and perhaps shows us that with continued testings and new findings, we can continue to refine our research, and come to new and more accurate conclusions.

Oldest Biomolecule Found?

An exceptionally preserved ichthyosaur fossil was discovered in Germany, with chemical traces of 180 million year old skin fragments and cells. These discoveries have lead to scientist believe that Ichthyosaurs may have had whale-like layers of blubber under their skin.

The blubber-like layer found in Ichthyosaurs would also have supported the idea that they were warm blooded, as blubber is commonly seen in many warm blooded marine animals today. Additional scientific testing on these fossils have also confirmed the presence of specialized cells that contain pigments called melanin. These pigments are also found in modern day marine animals that help disguise and regulate their body temperature.

The presence of modern day structures in the ancient Ichthyosaur provide insight into the developmental and evolutionary history of marine animals today. The cells from these fossils are one of the oldest cells ever found, and although they are under continued study today, their discovery has allowed scientist to develop deeper understandings of patterns and relationships between ancient and modern marine life; far more than fossil bones can.

A Key to Past Ecology?

Large concentrations of a well preserved species of Ichthyosaur were found in Torres del Paine National Park, Chile. This Ichthyosaur fossil site was significant because approximately 40 adult and juvenile Ichthyosaurs were buried together in groups, along with ammonites, belemnites, bivalves, and other fishes. The diversity and unique grouping of these fossils give valuable information to the conditions during the time of Ichthyosaurs and their behaviours.

Many of the other fossils found along side the Ichthyosaurs were characteristic of different fossil records of marine environments in the early Cretaceous period. These fossils provide support to the Cretaceous period, as zone fossils that were unique to certain paleoenvironments. The pairing of adult with juvenile Ichthyosaurs also suggested that they hunted together in groups for their prey. These fossil findings showed that viewing new fossil findings through the lense of paleontology allows one to more accurately inquire and formulate supported hypothesis about the past.
